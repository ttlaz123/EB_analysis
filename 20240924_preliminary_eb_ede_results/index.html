Early Dark Energy EB Spectrum Multicomponent Analysis

2024 September 24 — Tongtian Liu, Dominic Beck

Introduction

In Eskilt et al. ( Constraint on Early Dark Energy from Isotropic Cosmic Birefringence), the authors investigate the presence of Early Dark Energy (EDE) by analyzing its potential impact on the polarization of the CMB. They explore the possibility that the EDE field, \( \phi \), is a pseudoscalar "axionlike" field that interacts with electromagnetism in a parity-violating manner. The Lagrangian density for such a field is given by:

\[ \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2} (\partial \phi)^2 - V(\phi) - \frac{1}{4} F_{\mu\nu} F^{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{4} g \phi F_{\mu\nu} \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu}, \]

where \( g \) represents the photon-axion coupling constant. The term involving \( g \phi F_{\mu\nu} \tilde{F}^{\mu\nu} \) is a Chern-Simons term that breaks parity symmetry.

The authors use a "canonical" EDE potential:

\[ V(\phi) = V_0 [1 - \cos(\phi/f)]^3, \]

This model has been proposed as a solution to the Hubble tension. Additional constraints and alternative EDE models are discussed in the references listed in the paper.

They constrain the coupling constant \( g \) for pre-recombination Early Dark Energy (EDE), while accounting for post-recombination cosmic birefringence and miscalibration angles, \( \alpha + \beta \). To achieve this, the authors fit the stacked EB power spectrum for both \( g \) and \( \alpha + \beta \) simultaneously.

The analysis uses a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampler, emcee, to sample these parameters. The log-likelihood function is given by:

\[ -2 \ln L = \sum_b \frac{v_b^2}{\text{Var}(C_{EB,o}^b)}, \]

where \( v_b \) is defined as:

\[ v_b \equiv \bar{C}^{EB,o}_b - \cos[4(\alpha + \beta)] \, g M_{\text{Pl}} C^{EB,\text{EDE}}_b - \sin[4(\alpha + \beta)] \frac{1}{2} \left(C^{EE,\text{CMB}}_b - C^{BB,\text{CMB}}_b\right). \]

The CMB EE and BB power spectra are computed using CAMB with the best-fitting \(\Lambda\text{CDM}\) parameters from the Planck 2018 analysis. The EDE EB power spectrum is calculated using a modified version of the CLASS code, with \( g = M_{\text{Pl}}^{-1} \) as the coupling constant. The reduced Planck mass is approximately \( 2.4 \times 10^{18} \) GeV.

Using Planck's and SH0ES's polarization data, they investigated cosmic birefringence and its relation to EDE, calculating power spectra and sampling parameters with MCMC methods. Their goal was to differentiate between standard \(\Lambda\text{CDM}\) and EDE models. The study found \( g = (0.04 \pm 0.16) \, \text{M}^{-1}_{\text{Pl}} \) (68% CL).

We reproduce their findings and apply a similar methodology to analyze BICEP/Keck 18 (BK18) data, focusing on the EB spectra. Aligning with Eskilt’s approach, we account for post-recombination birefringence and polarization calibration angles, comparing BICEP data with their results. We utilize the BK18 polarization rotation analysis performed by Kenny with Cobaya MCMC to verify consistency, finding results aligned with Eskilt’s constraints on \( g \) and Kenny's polarization rotation angles.

Additionally, we extend the analysis by performing MCMC calculations on individual frequencies and conducting a multicomponent analysis combining several frequencies. We examine different bin ranges, using both 2-10 and 2-17 bin configurations to evaluate improvements in constraint precision.

Reproducing BK18 Polarization Rotation

This section reproduces Kenny's findings on BK18 polarization rotation using the Cobaya MCMC framework. The goal is to verify consistency with Kenny's results across various configurations and including different frequencies, binning schemes. The original analysis can be found in Kenny's Analysis Logbook .

The CMB signal can be modified by applying a uniform rotation. The modified spectrum is given by:

\[ C^{EB}_{\ell, expected} = \frac{1}{2} (C^{EE}_{\ell} - C^{BB}_{\ell}) \sin(4(\alpha + \beta)) \]

\(C^{EE}_{\ell}\) and \(C^{BB}_{\ell}\) are obtained from the summation of model_lens and model_dust with paths as stated in the appendix. To assess the fit of the modified spectra to observed data, we calculate the difference between the model expectation values \( C^{EB}_{b, \text{expected}} \) and the real bandpowers \( C^{EB}_{b, \text{observed}} \), where \( b \) denotes binned bandpowers over a range in \( \ell \). The \(\chi^2\) value is computed using the bandpower covariance matrix \( \text{C}_{EB} \):

\[ \chi^2 = ( C^{EB}_{b, \text{observed}} - C^{EB}_{b, \text{expected}} )^\top ( \text{C}_{EB} )^{-1} ( C^{EB}_{b, \text{observed}} - C^{EB}_{b, \text{expected}} ) \]

This analysis is similar to Eskilt's MCMC analysis where \( C^{EB, \text{EDE}}_b \) is set to zero, with a few differences: Eskilt zeros out all nondiagonal terms of the covariance matrix, while Kenny retains up to the second off-diagonal term. Eskilt uses MCMC for the analysis, whereas Kenny employs matmin to minimize the \(\chi^2\). Additionally, Eskilt uses around 70 bins up to \( \ell = 1500 \), whereas the BICEP spectra analysis uses less than 20 bins up to \( \ell = 500 \).

With the EDE set to zero, the results are within a hundredth of a degree of Kenny's results. Including extra bins does not change the results beyond a few hundredths of a degree, which is within the margin of error, suggesting a robust measurement. With EDE set to zero, the triangle plots show \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) roughly uniform, with the angle \( \alpha + \beta \) (aplusb) centered around the same polarization rotation calculated by Kenny.

The component spectra plots show that the EDE curve and the EE curve are quite similar up to \( \ell \) of 300, after which there is a more significant divergence. Because BB is much smaller than EE, its contribution to the parameters is negligible. Thus, with the EDE spectrum included in the analysis, using bins 2-10, the \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) term and \( \alpha + \beta \) term exhibit a negative correlation that cancels each other out, resulting in a very wide spread. However, once higher \( \ell \) bins are included, the spread for \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) dramatically decreases and centers around 0, suggesting that the EDE additions do not fit the data better than a simple polarization rotation.

Adding Early Dark Energy EB Spectrum and Simplified Multicomponent Analysis

We perform a simplified version of the multicomponent analysis on the 5 measured EB spectra from the 5 different frequency bands. We fit six parameters: the photon-axion coupling constant \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) and five different angles \( \text{aplusb}_{\text{freq}} \), one for each frequency band. Instead of including all the cross-spectra and covariances between the 5 different frequency bands, we sum the likelihood functions to quickly produce initial results.

When the EDE spectrum is set to zero, the results align with those from single component analyses with the EDE spectrum set to zero. Any deviations can be attributed to inherent variability in the MCMC software.

When the EDE spectrum is included, the triangle plots show \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) centered around -1.5, but with a large spread that is consistent with \( gM_{\text{Pl}} = 0 \). This is likely due to the similarity between the EDE and EE spectra at those \( \ell \) bins. As additional bins are added, \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) centers around 0 with a smaller spread. We obtain a sigma of 0.25, which is slightly larger than Eskilt's 0.16.

We perform the same MCMC analysis on the 499 simulations using the simplified multicomponent approach. Again, using only bins 2-10 results in a greater spread for every variable due to the similarity between the EDE EB curve and the EE curve, which can cancel each other out with a negative correlation.

By plotting the peaks of all simulations, we observe that the parameters are centered around 0 for both \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) and each polarization rotation angle. This aligns with simulations that do not include Early Dark Energy or polarization rotations.

For both bins 2-10 and 2-17, the measured EB spectra differ from the simulations in the polarization rotation but are consistent in \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \). This discrepancy is due to the real data being measured by detectors that likely have some inherent polarization rotation. However, since the \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \) results align with simulations that exclude Early Dark Energy, we further constrain the parameter \( gM_{\text{Pl}} \), supporting Eskilt's result that it is consistent with zero.

Next Steps

  1. Investigate the Discrepancy in Peak Spreads:

    We still need to understand why the peak spreads in the simulated data are significantly wider than the spread of a single realization. This discrepancy might indicate a problem with the covariance calculations. Understanding whether this discrepancy impacts the overall analysis and its conclusions will help in making necessary adjustments to improve the fidelity of the analysis.

  2. Perform Full Multicomponent Analysis:

    A full multicomponent analysis involves analyzing all available cross-spectra to capture the complete picture of the polarization data. By including all the cross-spectra in the analysis and calculating the full covariance matrix, we can better account for the interactions between different frequencies.

  3. Combine BICEP Data with Planck and Other Spectra:

    Integrating the BICEP data with Planck and other spectral datasets will help us obtain a more robust and accurate representation of the polarization signals. This combined approach will help in cross-validating results, reducing uncertainties, and enhancing the overall reliability of our conclusions.
